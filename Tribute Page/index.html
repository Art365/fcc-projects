Carl Edward Sagan was an American astronomer, cosmologist, astrophysicist, astrobiologist, author, science popularizer, and science communicator. Sagan published over 600 scientific papers and articles and was an author or contributor of more than 20 books. Some of his best-known books include Cosmos, The Demon-Haunted World, The Dragons of Eden, and Pale Blue Dot. Sagan also co-wrote and narrated the award-winning 1980 television series Cosmos: A Personal Voyage, which is the most widely watched series in the history of American public television. Sagan advocated scientific skeptical inquiry and the scientific method, pioneered exobiology and promoted the Search for Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence (SETI).

Source: NASA JPL. The Sounds of Earth

The golden record shown above was assembled by Sagan and was later launched into space aboard the Voyager spacecraft. It contains greetings in 60 languages, samples of music from different cultures and eras, and natural and man-made sounds from Earth; messages that could potentially be understood by intelligent extraterrestrials should they ever find them.

Source: NASA JPL. Pale Blue Dot

Photo of Earth taken by the Voyager 1 spacecraft, at Sagan's request, from a distance of more than 4 billion miles. The Earth is the tiny dot approximately halfway down the ray of light on the right.

Sagan wrote of this photo in his Pale Blue Dot book:


"Look again at that dot. That's here. That's home. That's us. On it everyone you love, everyone you know, everyone you ever heard of, every human being who ever was, lived out their lives. The aggregate of our joy and suffering, thousands of confident religions, ideologies, and economic doctrines, every hunter and forager, every hero and coward, every creator and destroyer of civilization, every king and peasant, every young couple in love, every mother and father, hopeful child, inventor and explorer, every teacher of morals, every corrupt politician, every "superstar," every "supreme leader," every saint and sinner in the history of our species lived there-on a mote of dust suspended in a sunbeam. ... There is perhaps no better demonstration of the folly of human conceits than this distant image of our tiny world. To me, it underscores our responsibility to deal more kindly with one another, and to preserve and cherish the pale blue dot, the only home we've ever known."
